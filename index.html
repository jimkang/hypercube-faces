The hypercube is a 4-dimensional cube. You may have seen visualizations of it that look like this:

The tesseract image is useful for showing you how the vertices in a hypercube can connect to form 8 different 3D cubes. (There's the inner cube, the outer cube, then 6 cubes connecting the inner cube to the outer cube.) Without context, this image can be misleading. The 6 connecting cubes are drawn using lines that A) are not at right angles to each other and B) appear to be lines in 3D space. A casual scroller might think, oh, the hypercube is just two nested cubes with lines connecting them.

But that's not what it is.

The hypercube has "depth" in a fourth dimension which is imaginary. (AFAIK; please let me know if the fourth dimension has been proven to be real.) Even if it is not imaginary, we can't see it because we can only perceive 3D. The lines moving along that fourth dimension (assuming the hypercube we're talking about has edges parallel to the x, y, z, and w axes) are actually perpendicular to edges along the first three dimensions (where perpendicularity is tested by getting the dot product of the edges that might be perpendicular to each other and verifying that the dot product is 0).

So, what could we actually perceive about a hypercube?

If you do the Flatland thing and think about someone that can only perceive 2D, what could they perceive about a 3D cube? They could see the faces as squares when the cube is in certain "cardinal" positions (edges parallel to axes). So, we 3D perceivers could can see the faces of a hypercube as cubes when it is in cardinal positions.

It's analagous to the hypersquare, which we usually think of as "traditional" cubes. Just as all of the faces of the hypersquare are squares, all of the faces of the hypercube are (traditional) cubes. These cube faces are 3D, which is different from the 2D faces we're used to think of when we think of traditional cubes, so we have to think of what we mean by a face.
